## From Biology to Bioinformatics: A Journey I Never Planned

My journey into bioinformatics was not planned. In fact, I started from a place of complete unfamiliarity with technology. I was someone who was comfortable with biology but had little to no exposure to programming or computational skills. Technology always felt like something risky - something I was not meant for.

Coming from a biology background, my world was very different. It was the one subject that always made sense to me. I could spend hours studying human physiology or genetics without getting tired. But I never imagined that one day, this same interest would push me into a field that combines biology with programming - something I once feared the most.

Looking back, it feels strange how life works. 
>The things we avoid the most become the very things that shape us. 

This is my journey - how I went from a biology-focused student who feared technology to building a path in bioinformatics.


## The Beginning: A Typical Dream

From a very young age, I have always been a hardworking child. This might have come from my father, who himself is a teacher. Among all the subjects, biology was my favorite. It came very naturally to me; I never felt burdened while studying it. I was especially fascinated by human physiology and diseases. And like many kids, I grew up with the dream of becoming a doctor, a dream that was both mine and my family’s.

Like most students in India, I also appeared for the NEET exam. But at that time, I had never really prepared for a competitive exam of this level. I was focused on my board exams and just wanted to understand how these national-level exams worked. As expected, the result was not what I had imagined. It was one of those moments where expectations meet reality, and you realize that hard work alone is not enough without the right strategy.


## College Changed Everything: When Biology Became More Than Just a Subject

After my NEET attempt, I took admission to B.Sc. Zoology (Hons.) at the University of Delhi. At that time, I still thought that I might prepare again for NEET while continuing college, keeping both options open. However, once my classes began, something unexpected happened: my entire perspective towards biology began to change.

This was very different from what I had studied in school. It was no longer about memorizing; it was about understanding, questioning, and exploring. I was exposed to topics in much greater depth, and for the first time, I felt like I was actually learning biology, not just reading it from books. That curiosity pulled me in more than anything else.

Within the first few months of college, I got the opportunity to work on a research project under the DBT Star College Scheme. I also participated in international conferences organized by my college. Somewhere during this time, without even realizing it, my goal started to shift. I was no longer thinking about becoming a doctor. I had started seeing myself as someone who wanted to do research. That was the moment I truly felt that I had discovered what real biology is.


## Facing My Biggest Fear

Up until this point, my world was limited to biology. But not everything goes as you imagine. I never knew that my love for biology would make me face one of the biggest fears of my life -computers. I had never really interacted with technology beyond basic things like using Word or making presentations. Even simple tasks like downloading software or making online payments felt intimidating to me.

>*"What if I click on the wrong link? What if my laptop gets damaged? What if something goes wrong?"*

These thoughts were always running at the back of my mind. I avoided these things as much as I could, almost like walking on eggshells around technology.

But destiny has its own plans. I still remember during my evolutionary biology course, we were asked to install PHYLIP and MEGA for sequence analysis. For most students, it was just another assignment, but for me, it felt like stepping into an uncharted territory. I was scared to even click on the link. At the same time, I could see my classmates working on it so easily, while I was just sitting there, looking at others' systems, trying to copy their moves so that I could somehow get the task done.

At that time, I felt completely lost, like a fish out of water. Totally embarrassed, I didn’t even dare to ask my classmates. It was not just confusion, it was fear mixed with embarrassment.

But that embarrassment also made me realize something important.
*"How long will I keep running away from this?*
>*At some point, I have to face it."*

That was the first time I consciously decided to face my fear of technology.


## The Night I Took My First Step

After reaching home, I decided to take a small step. With a lot of hesitation, but also a little courage, I opened YouTube, searched for Phylip, and went through a dozen of videos. I found one with easy-to-follow steps and started copying them one by one.

Every step felt like a slow climb uphill: pausing, replaying, pausing, and then replaying again. It was slow, but this time I was determined not to give up.

Once the program was completely installed and working properly, I felt a huge sense of relief and a quiet sense of pride, as if I had just crossed a mountain of my own fears. (I repeated the same process for the other tool as well; I must say that using Phylip was a bit more difficult than MEGA.)

I still remember that moment, I looked at the clock, and it was almost half past midnight. I was completely exhausted, but I felt a kind of excitement I had never experienced before. This happiness was something different, something deeper than just completing an assignment.

It may seem like a very small achievement, but for me, it was a turning point, a moment that changed how I saw myself. For the first time, I realized, maybe I can actually do this.
>Sometimes the smallest step becomes the biggest turning point.


## The Moment I Discovered Bioinformatics

But that wasn’t the end of the story; the real turning point was yet to come. The real discovery started in one of our biotechnology classes.

Our professor mentioned something that was going to change my entire direction. In a class about microarray analysis, he started talking about his Ph.D., and a word came out of his mouth - the R language. Although I didn’t fully understand it at the time, it sparked my curiosity.

Later, I spoke to him to understand more about it, and that conversation introduced me to a completely new perspective. That discussion changed the way I looked at biology.

He explained how biological research is evolving, how large amounts of data are being generated, and how there is a growing need for people who can analyze and interpret that data using computational tools. For the first time, I understood that biology was no longer limited to experiments, physiology, or genetics alone; it was becoming increasingly data-driven.

This idea stayed with me; it lingered in my mind long after the day ended. After my exams, I started exploring more on my own and came across the field of bioinformatics. As I read about it, everything slowly started to connect - biology, data, and technology coming together like pieces of a puzzle.

The more I read, the more it made sense. It felt like a natural extension of what I already loved, but with a whole new dimension.

I also began thinking more practically about my career. I realized that traditional paths might take longer and be more uncertain, while this field opened up multiple possibilities. That was the moment I decided to shift towards bioinformatics.


## The Phase That Broke Me Before It Built Me

After deciding to pursue bioinformatics, I applied to different universities, but things didn’t go as smoothly as I had hoped. I missed some deadlines, and even where I had good scores, I couldn’t secure admission on time. Eventually, through a spot round, I got admission into M.Sc. Bioinformatics program at the Central University of Punjab.

But by the time I joined, most of the first semester had already been completed. Classes were almost over, and exams were approaching. I had missed the basics, and now I had to prepare for the semester exams.

I had no background in bioinformatics, and suddenly I was expected to catch up with everything all at once. It was overwhelming, to say the least. Along with that, I was also struggling with my health due to the extreme weather conditions, which made it even harder to focus.

At some point, everything started to feel like too much: confusion, frustration, and constant pressure piling up all at once. I realized that I was not able to cope with the situation, and I made the most difficult decision: to drop out and return home.

It wasn’t an easy choice; it felt like taking a step back when all I wanted was to move forward. It was not what I had planned, but it was what I needed at that moment.
>Sometimes stepping back is the only way to move forward.


## Learning to Take Control of My Own Journey

After coming back home, I had a lot of time to think. Initially, it felt like everything had come to a standstill, but slowly I started to reflect on what had actually gone wrong. I realized that the problem was my approach. I had expected that I would learn everything through classes, but that does not work when you step into higher education.

I understood that if I really wanted to learn this, I would have to take responsibility for it myself.

I started looking for resources online and came across some courses on Coursera. At the same time, I began learning through YouTube. After some guidance, I decided to start with Python. This was not a one-day decision - I chose Python as my first programming language because it was beginner-friendly and widely used in the field.

It was not easy in the beginning, but perseverance slowly started to pay off, and with each small step, things began to fall into place. This phase completely changed the way I approached learning. It taught me that consistency matters more than speed, and that learning is a gradual process, not something that happens overnight.


## The Comeback: A Second Chance

Along with self-learning, I was also preparing for the entrance exams again. This time, my approach was different; I was clearer about what I wanted and how I needed to prepare.

I appeared for the entrance exam for Jamia Millia Islamia, and this time, things worked out in my favor. I secured the first rank and got admission into the M.Sc. Bioinformatics program in the Department of Computer Science.

It marked a new beginning for me - a fresh start where I could rebuild myself with a stronger mindset and clearer direction.
>This time, I wasn’t starting from scratch — I was starting from experience.


## Growing, Learning, and Finding My Place

At Jamia, my learning became more structured and consistent, and I continued to build my understanding step by step. Instead of trying to learn everything at once, I focused on strengthening one concept at a time, which made a big difference.

Along with academics, I also explored learning beyond the classroom through online resources and internships at different institutes, where I gained hands-on experience. This helped me connect theory with practical applications and understand the field better. I also started connecting with more people in the field, learning from their experiences and broadening my perspective.

I got introduced to machine learning and artificial intelligence, and began to understand how these areas are shaping modern healthcare systems. Slowly, my confidence started to grow, and I began to feel more comfortable in a field that once felt completely unfamiliar.

And today, I stand here with a completely different mindset, someone who once feared technology is now building a path in it. I know I still have a long way to go, and there is so much more to learn, but now I am no longer afraid of the journey. Instead, I am excited for what lies ahead.


## What This Journey Taught Me

When I look back, I realize that this journey has taught me much more than just technical skills.

First, it’s completely okay to not know everything in the beginning. I started from a point where even doing basic things felt difficult, but that doesn’t mean you cannot learn. Everyone starts somewhere - every expert was once a beginner.

Second, learning takes time. There were moments when I felt like I was not making any progress, but understanding things step by step is far more important than rushing through them. Slow and steady truly wins the race here.

Third, failure is a part of the process. Dropping out was not something I had planned, but it taught me a lot about myself and how to approach things differently. Sometimes, what feels like a setback is actually setting the stage for a stronger comeback.

Fourth, self-learning is very powerful. At some point, you have to take responsibility for your own learning instead of depending on others. And believe me, self-learning is one of the most rewarding parts of the journey: it builds both confidence and independence.

And most importantly, growth does not happen overnight. It happens slowly, through small, consistent steps, and those small steps eventually lead to big changes.


## To Anyone Standing Where I Once Stood

If you are someone who comes from a biology background and feels unsure about stepping into fields like bioinformatics, I just want to say, you’re not alone. I have been there, feeling confused, scared, and not knowing where to start.

It might feel overwhelming in the beginning, especially if technology is new to you. But with time, patience, and consistent effort, things do start to fall into place. You don’t need to know everything from day one, just take one step at a time.

Everyone’s journey is different, but if you are willing to learn and take that first step, you will eventually find your own path.

>This is just the beginning, and your story is still waiting to be written.
